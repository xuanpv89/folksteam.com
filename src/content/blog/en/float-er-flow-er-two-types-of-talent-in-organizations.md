Float-er & Flow-er: Two Types of Talent in Organizations
A few years ago, while job hunting, I received a rather unusual offer. The role didn’t have a fixed title. In fact, it involved taking on three different positions. There was no clear job description either. The expectation was simple: rotate between roles after a period of time.

At that time, I found it exciting. After spending years in a single specialization and wanting a change, this “do a bit of everything” role felt refreshing.

Looking back now, this kind of role is no longer unusual in today’s job market. It even has a name: Float-er.

So what exactly is a Float-er? Why do organizations need them? And what do we call those who are not Float-ers?

Based on my personal perspective, I’d like to describe two increasingly common types of talent in organizations: Float-ers and Flow-ers.

1. What is a Float-er?
A Float-er is someone without a fixed specialization. They move between roles, sometimes handling multiple responsibilities at once. Within an organization, they are flexible enough to temporarily fill gaps, take on new projects, or bring fresh perspectives to teams by thinking “outside the specialty.”

Strengths of Float-ers:
Big-picture thinking and adaptability

Quick to respond to change—and often drive it

Less constrained by rigid or outdated thinking

Strong understanding of cross-functional collaboration

Willingness to learn and switch domains

Less likely to get entangled in office politics

Weaknesses of Float-ers:
Lack of deep expertise in a specific area

May not fully grasp the root causes of complex problems

Limited understanding of long-term trends within a field

Can get bored with repetition

Sometimes push for change without fully understanding internal dynamics

Who is suited to be a Float-er?
People with strong ownership mindset

Curious, adaptable, and open to learning

Those who enjoy change and uncertainty

A few notes on Float-ers:
Early in their careers, they may start by filling gaps or rotating roles

Over time, they may transition into management, operations, or project leadership

Some may eventually specialize and become Flow-ers, while others treat “being a Float-er” as their core expertise

As the name suggests, they are like “clouds”—fluid, mobile, and sometimes unpredictable

2. What is a Flow-er?
A Flow-er is someone who follows a defined specialization path. They usually stay in one domain for a long time, progressing from Fresher → Junior → Senior → Expert.

Strengths of Flow-ers:
Deep expertise and strong domain knowledge

Clear understanding of industry trends and historical context

Mastery built through long-term focus and experience

Weaknesses of Flow-ers:
Difficult to transition outside their core specialization

Limited exposure to other domains

May need to start over when changing careers

Who is suited to be a Flow-er?
People who discover their strengths early in a specific field

Those who value stability and deep learning

Individuals who prefer mastery over variety

A few notes on Flow-ers:
They can switch careers, but their nature remains the same: focusing deeply on one path at a time

Like the wordplay suggests, they are like “flowers”—growing in a linear, structured lifecycle

Or like a “flow”—moving within defined boundaries, gradually forming something bigger over time

3. Organizations Need Both Float-ers and Flow-ers
Neither type is inherently better than the other. They simply serve different purposes.

An organization made up only of Flow-ers may become rigid and resistant to change.
An organization made up only of Float-ers may lack depth and stability.

The key is balance.

By combining both types, organizations can leverage:

The depth and expertise of Flow-ers

The flexibility and innovation of Float-ers

A truly effective organization doesn’t just “flow”—it also needs to “float.”
