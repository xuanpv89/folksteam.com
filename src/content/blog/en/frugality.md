These days, I’ve been reading Into the Wild at a very slow pace, yet somehow the stories of survival and the search for meaning feel strangely close to the rhythm of lockdown life.

This morning, when I took out the rice cooker to prepare a new meal, I realized I had wasted a fair amount of leftover rice from the night before. I couldn’t finish it, it spoiled, and I threw it away. This isn’t unusual—I’ve done the same thing hundreds of times, driven by a childhood habit of “better too much than too little.” But this time, it felt completely different.

I suddenly regretted those leftovers as if I had lost something valuable. And when I connected that feeling to the past few days, I noticed something had changed. Never before have I been so careful with every grain of rice. I thought about the times I carefully scraped the last grains from a bag of rice bought at the supermarket, the moments I picked through every vegetable stem, or tried to salvage a cracked egg… And I couldn’t help but blame myself a little for having wasted so much before, compared to those careful moments.

Perhaps I belong to a generation for whom true scarcity—the kind that twists your gut with hunger—feels distant. If it exists at all, it’s something from childhood memories or stories passed down by older generations. Stories of running out of rice and borrowing a canister’s worth from neighbors, of sharing a few eggs among a whole family, of meals that consisted of nothing but boiled water spinach. Not to mention even earlier times, when people ate mixed-grain rice or literally died of hunger. The lesson of frugality has mostly remained theoretical, because real, survival-level deprivation still feels far away.

But during lockdown, when we had to scramble for food—supermarkets closed, local shops cordoned off, vegetable prices skyrocketing—things felt different. After cooking, looking at those bright green boiled vegetables, taking small bites, I could suddenly taste how sweet they were. Precious, in a way I had never felt before.

It makes me think: beyond all the hardship people talk about, this pandemic has also given us a chance to come a little closer to the struggles of previous generations. Those stories of rationed meals and hunger are no longer just abstract—they resemble, even faintly, the conditions of wartime or curfews. What used to be theory has, in some sense, become experience.

Suddenly, I remember when I was a child, my mother would make me turn leftover rice into mẻ—a kind of fermented rice used as a souring agent in Northern Vietnamese cooking.

Back then, I found it tedious and unnecessary. Now, I see it for what it is: a clever, thoughtful invention.

I need to be more frugal.'

“On January 16, 1991, he went thirty-six days without meeting or speaking to anyone. During that time, McCandless survived on just two kilos of rice and some sea creatures he gathered himself. The experience later convinced him that he could live in the Alaskan wilderness on a similarly meager diet.”

These days, I’ve been reading Into the Wild at a very slow pace, yet somehow the stories of survival and the search for meaning feel strangely close to the rhythm of lockdown life.

This morning, when I took out the rice cooker to prepare a new meal, I realized I had wasted a fair amount of leftover rice from the night before. I couldn’t finish it, it spoiled, and I threw it away. This isn’t unusual—I’ve done the same thing hundreds of times, driven by a childhood habit of “better too much than too little.” But this time, it felt completely different.

I suddenly regretted those leftovers as if I had lost something valuable. And when I connected that feeling to the past few days, I noticed something had changed. Never before have I been so careful with every grain of rice. I thought about the times I carefully scraped the last grains from a bag of rice bought at the supermarket, the moments I picked through every vegetable stem, or tried to salvage a cracked egg… And I couldn’t help but blame myself a little for having wasted so much before, compared to those careful moments.

Perhaps I belong to a generation for whom true scarcity—the kind that twists your gut with hunger—feels distant. If it exists at all, it’s something from childhood memories or stories passed down by older generations. Stories of running out of rice and borrowing a canister’s worth from neighbors, of sharing a few eggs among a whole family, of meals that consisted of nothing but boiled water spinach. Not to mention even earlier times, when people ate mixed-grain rice or literally died of hunger. The lesson of frugality has mostly remained theoretical, because real, survival-level deprivation still feels far away.

But during lockdown, when we had to scramble for food—supermarkets closed, local shops cordoned off, vegetable prices skyrocketing—things felt different. After cooking, looking at those bright green boiled vegetables, taking small bites, I could suddenly taste how sweet they were. Precious, in a way I had never felt before.

It makes me think: beyond all the hardship people talk about, this pandemic has also given us a chance to come a little closer to the struggles of previous generations. Those stories of rationed meals and hunger are no longer just abstract—they resemble, even faintly, the conditions of wartime or curfews. What used to be theory has, in some sense, become experience.

Suddenly, I remember when I was a child, my mother would make me turn leftover rice into mẻ—a kind of fermented rice used as a souring agent in Northern Vietnamese cooking.

Back then, I found it tedious and unnecessary. Now, I see it for what it is: a clever, thoughtful invention.

I need to be more frugal.
