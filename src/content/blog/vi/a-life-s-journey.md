“No, no, Mom… I was already happy, you were already happy! Everyone was already happy…” — Liên Anh’s words came out half-strangled, as if every ounce of strength in her throat was trying to hold back the surge of sobs rising from deep within her chest; half like a scream she forced herself to restrain, shaping it instead into something intelligible. Through the slightly ajar door, people heard those muffled, heavy words, a brief stillness — “Mom… Mom… Mom…” — followed by a series of violent, choking sobs.

Everyone rushed into the room. Only two bodies remained: two women — one collapsed to the side, hair scattered across the wooden floor; the other lying still, as if asleep. A faint breeze lifted the pale purple curtain by the window on the twenty-first floor.

One had died. One had come back to life.

**

The funeral was simple, swift, as such things often are. A week passed, and everything seemed to slip back into its usual flow. Everyone had work to do, lives to continue. Only this apartment had changed — now with an altar thick with curling incense smoke, and without a mother, a wife.

**

In the living room, the scent of freshly burned incense lingered. The yellow light from a shaded lamp in the corner mixed with the red glow of the altar, casting a dim heaviness over every face in the house. Tomorrow, Liên Anh would return to Đà Lạt.

They sat in silence around the wooden table, sometimes glancing at one another, sometimes toward the altar, sometimes absentmindedly out the window. Mr. Vũ looked at Liên Anh and asked:

“Are you really set on this?”

“I thought you and my brother and sister had already agreed.”

“Should I come with you?”

“I think you should stay home and tend the incense for Mom. That’s the best way to keep a spiritual connection between you and her. On full-moon days and the first of the month, you and my brother can come visit her at the pagoda with me. I’ll come back for the forty-ninth day ceremony.”

“…Alright then.” Mr. Vũ gazed vaguely toward the front door, then suddenly turned back to her. “What did your mother say to you?”

Liên Anh looked deeply into his eyes, stayed silent for a moment, then said, “I’ll tell you and my brother when I come back.”

**

On the morning Liên Anh left, Hà Nội was suddenly covered in a veil of white mist. She woke early, wrapped herself in her mother’s wool scarf, made a cup of warm tea, and pulled a rattan chair close to the window, staring into the distance. She had slept only three hours the night before.

From the twenty-first floor, the whole city looked blurred, as though a thin layer of vapor had been smeared across it — like the fogged mirror in a bathroom on winter days. The streets were quiet; the almond and sưa trees stood bare of leaves. The air was already heavy with sadness, now soaked even deeper in it.

She nudged the window open just a little, letting the cold air slip in. Pulling the scarf closer, she cupped the cooling tea in both hands, lifting it to her lips without drinking. Suddenly, a tear welled up and rolled down from the corner of her eye — the first tear since her mother passed. It quickly dried, leaving a faint trace on her cheek.

She stood up and opened all the windows. The wind lifted the white lace curtains.

She washed a white porcelain vase, arranged sprigs of white asters in it, and placed them on the altar. She didn’t light incense — she only looked deeply into her mother’s eyes in the photograph.

“If Mom were still alive, she’d probably say, ‘Just making a mess of the house,’” Liên Anh thought.

She knew her mother didn’t like flowers. But she still wanted to put them there. Or perhaps not — she wasn’t entirely sure. Sometimes what her mother said was just that — words. What she truly felt took much longer to understand.

Like the first time Liên Anh ever earned money — winning a school prize in eighth grade — she bought her mother a picture frame. Her mother’s words had almost made her cry: “Such fuss. I only like things you can eat, or money.” She had been sad for days. And yet later, she often saw her mother take it out, look at it, clean it carefully.

As if saying she liked it would spoil her children. Or perhaps admitting she cared for such sentimental things felt out of place with her circumstances. After all, her life had been consumed by the dizzying struggle for money — where was the space for a spiritual life?

**

At twenty, she married — her first love, and her only one. The farthest journey she had taken in those twenty years was from Nghệ An to Hà Nam for her wedding.

At thirty, with two children aged ten and five, Mr. Vũ suggested a family trip. She said, “Let’s wait until we’re a bit more stable financially. Things are still hard.”

At thirty-five, when things were better, she said, “Let’s wait until Tùng Anh gets into high school.”

At forty, when he did, she said, “Let’s first get Liên Anh into a good school, and Tùng Anh into university. Then we’ll be free to travel.”

At forty-five, the family moved to Hà Nội for Mr. Vũ’s job. They became entangled in loans to pay off a modest house on the outskirts. For years, they remained confined within that narrow life.

At fifty, Tùng Anh was married, Liên Anh in university. Their old house had deteriorated, so the couple struggled to save and build a three-story home. Again, they were tied down by a house.

At sixty, she passed away.

Her farthest journey had been from Nghệ An to Hà Nội.
Her longest road had been from Hà Nội back to Nghệ An.
Her deepest journey was the journey of her life.
